Commercial Launch Growth Surges Ahead of Government

In the 1980s, the US permitted cheaper, mass-produced commercial satellites to be produced which rapidly overtook the costly, infrequently launched government satellites by volume

Commercial launch growth rate now exceeds government, driven by rising demand for global internet, Earth imagery, agriculture, and navigation

Satellites got cheaper — but rockets didn’t, until reusable rockets arrived in the 2010s...

Rocket Reusability

By pioneering reusable launch technology, SpaceX's Falcon 9 rocket was able to deploy their Starlink satellites at unmatched scale, making up almost half of all satellites today.

Reusable rocket technology made cost to launch the commercial satellites cheaper, resulting in a spike in satellites launched by rockets

Not only are satellites and launch costs falling, but satellites are also becoming more efficient—lighter-weight designs use less power and are cheaper to build.

Less mass. Less power. Lower cost. That’s the commercial advantage.

Satellite Efficiency

With time, commercial satellites have become lighter, requiring less power to operate, and cheaper to manufacture, enabling mass production

The explosion in satellite numbers is the direct result of:

1. Regulatory loosening to commercialize satellite manufacturing leading to mass manufacturing
2. Reusable rockets lowered launch costs
3. Lighter satellites reduced power needs and overall costs

But rapid growth is turning orbit into a traffic jam…

Orbital Congestion Crisis

Most small, mass-produced satellites crowd into Low Earth Orbit, where limited space creates the Kessler Syndrome — rising collision risks and growing space debris

Below is a scaled down visualization of the exact trajectory of the satellites, highlighting growing congestion, especially in the Lower Earth Orbit

Damages from collisions can create thousands of debris fragments, each capable of damaging or destroying other satellites, leading to a cascading effect that endangers all space operations

Sustainability in the Stars

To keep orbit usable, we need sustainable practices—deorbiting, collision avoidance, and better constellation design. Astroscale, ClearSpace, and LeoLabs lead debris removal, but our choices today will determine whether space remains a frontier of opportunity or becomes an unusable junkyard.
