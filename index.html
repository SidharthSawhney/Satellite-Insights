Government vs Commercial Ownership

In the 1980s, the U.S. enabled commercial satellite launches, shifting from large, costly defense satellites to smaller, cheaper, mass-produced ones providing real-time communication, earth observation, navigation, and agriculture data.

Although satellites grew cheaper and smaller, launches stayed costly until reusable rockets arrived in the 2010s, slashing costs

Launch Vehicle Dominance Over Time

Given around 50% of all satellites in space are SpaceX's communication Starlink satellites, it's no surprise that SpaceX's Falcon 9 rocket has dominated launches since it became operational in Dec 2015.

Not only are satellites and launch costs falling, but satellites are also becoming more efficient—lighter-weight designs use less power and are cheaper to build.

Satellite Efficiency

The lighter the satellite, the less power it needs to operate, and the cheaper it is to launch

The explosion in satellite numbers is the direct result of:

1. Regulatory loosening to commercialize satellite manufacturing leading to mass manufacturing
2. Reusable rockets lowered launch costs
3. Lighter satellites reduced power needs and overall costs

But this rapid growth comes with a hidden cost...

Orbital Congestion Crisis

Most lightweight, mass produced satellites have applications which are best suited in the Lower Earth Orbits given current technology. This leads to the Kessler Syndrome - where the risk of collisions among satellites increases exponentially, resulting in space debris

Sustainability in the Stars

As we enter this new era of space utilization, sustainable practices are no longer optional. International cooperation on satellite deorbiting, anti-collision technologies, responsible constellation management is essential to keep orbit accessible for future generations. Companies like Astroscale, ClearSpace, and LeoLabs are pioneering debris removal. The choices we make today will determine whether space remains a frontier of opportunity or becomes an unusable junkyard.
